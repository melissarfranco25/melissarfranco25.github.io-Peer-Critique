Over the past years anxiety has become a prevalent concern across all age groups. Many of the factors that contribute to anxiety attacks are diverse but certain lifestyle choices such as caffeine intake are increasingly being blamed. This research aims to explore the following question:

Is There a Relationship Between Caffeine Intake, Alcohol Consumption, and Physical Activity to the Severity of Anxiety Attacks Among Different Age Groups (Young Adults, Adults, and Seniors)?

Anxiety Attack Factors Symptoms and Severity is the Kaggle dataset I selected for this project. This dataset has 12,000 rows with many factors related to anxiety attacks. Designed to explore patterns, triggers, and potential correlations in anxiety disorders. In total, this dataset has a total of 20 factors including lifestyle habits, stress levels, major life events, caffeine intake, and heart rate.

Storytelling

Our initial bar plot revealed the average severity of anxiety attacks across different age groups. It was clear that all age groups fell within a similar range of 5-6 on a scale of 1 to 10, with Seniors slightly lower and Young Adults slightly higher in severity.

Next, we focused to caffeine intake. The bar plot used showed average caffeine intake by age group highlighted that Adults consumed the least caffeine, with Young Adults at the top and Seniors in the middle. From this visualization, I identified a trend of Young Adults consuming the most caffeine seemed consistent with their higher severity of anxiety attacks.

Diving deeper, I used a facet grid to break down caffeine consumption into over and under-average groups. This revealed a surprising insight: Adults had the highest number of individuals consuming over-average caffeine, followed by Seniors and then Young Adults. This finding tested my initial theory, suggesting that other factors might influence anxiety severity in Young Adults.

Finally, a line plot offered a fresh perspective on the relationship between these variables. I observed that for Adults and Seniors, the severity of anxiety attacks decreased with below-average caffeine intake, indicating a potential benefit of lower caffeine consumption. However, for Young Adults, the severity of anxiety attacks slightly increased with below-average caffeine intake, further complicating our initial assumptions.

I partially answered my inital question. I was able to identify relationships between caffeine intake and anxiety severity across different age groups. While the Young Adults group initially seemed to support my theory, further analysis showed that their anxiety severity didn't significantly differ with caffeine consumption levels. In contrast, for Seniors, higher caffeine intake correlated with increased anxiety severity, which could suggest a heightened sensitivity to caffeine in this age group.

Impact

Analyzing these questions can help bring a better understanding of the relationship between factors and could aid healthcare providers in giving personalized recommendations. Such as how much caffeine consumption based on age can help manage anxiety symptoms better? Or help in building tailored treatment plans based on individual needs. However, there are still some possible risks like inaccurate conclusions due to individual differences in metabolism and other health conditions. Along with the risk of interpreting therapy or medication alone determines anxiety levels while disregarding other factors like a support system.

Missing data is also a big concern since we do not know the duration or type of physical activity as well as the source of caffeine which would be useful for an in depth analysis. For health issues, we do not have data on pre-existing heart conditions or exercise routine which can sway the conclusions in a way that does not apply to everyday patients.
